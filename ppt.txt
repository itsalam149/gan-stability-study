Title: GAN-Based Image Generation: From Random Noise to Controlled Synthesis
Bullets:
- Investigating Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs).
- Evaluating architectures: Vanilla GAN, DCGAN, and conditional DCGAN (cDCGAN).
- Datasets: MNIST and Fashion-MNIST.

Speaker Notes: "Welcome to my presentation. Today I will discuss my deep learning project on Generative Adversarial Networks. Instead of just implementing a basic GAN, I tracked the architectural evolution of GANs from simple MLPs to complex conditional convolutions, and measured their performance mathematically."

SLIDE 2
Title: What is a GAN? The Zero-Sum Game
Bullets:
- Generator: Transforms random noise (z) into fake images.
- Discriminator: Evaluates images and predicts 'Real' or 'Fake'.
- Adversarial Training: They train simultaneously in a minimax game.
- The Goal: The Generator successfully fools the Discriminator.
Speaker Notes: "A GAN consists of two neural networks fighting each other. The Generator acts like a counterfeiter, while the Discriminator acts like a detective. Over time, the counterfeiter gets so good that the detective can no longer tell the difference between real and fake data."

SLIDE 3
Title: The Project Pipeline & Methodology
Bullets:
- Phase 1 (Setup): Normalizing datasets (MNIST / Fashion-MNIST) to [-1,1] and initializing configs.
- Phase 2 (Training): Iterative adversarial loops updating weights via Backpropagation (BCE Loss).
- Phase 3 (Output): Generating 10,000 final images and computing structural FID scores.
Speaker Notes: "Before discussing the models, here is the overarching pipeline of the system. First, the data is loaded and mathematically normalized. During training, the Generator and Discriminator constantly update each other's weights through backpropagation. Finally, instead of just eyeballing the results, the system exports 10,000 images and uses an Inception neural network to mathematically score the output."

SLIDE 4
Title: Model 1: Vanilla GAN (The Baseline)
Bullets:
- Architecture: Fully-connected Linear layers (Multi-Layer Perceptron).
- Dataset: MNIST (Handwritten digits).
- Result: Recognizable digits, but highly blurry and noisy.
- Limitation: Linear layers do not understand 2D spatial geometry.
Speaker Notes: "We started with the original Vanilla GAN. Because it uses linear layers, it flattens the image and treats pixels independently. While it successfully generated digits, they were fuzzy. This proved that we needed an architecture that understands spatial relationships."

SLIDE 5
Title: Model 2: DCGAN (Deep Convolutional GAN)
Bullets:
- Architecture: Replaced Linear layers with Convolutional and Transposed Convolutional layers.
- Upgrade: Uses BatchNorm and LeakyReLU for stability.
- Result: Exceptionally sharp, high-contrast images.
Speaker Notes: "To fix the blurriness, we built a DCGAN. By using Convolutions, the network learned edges, curves, and shapes instead of just raw pixels. The visual difference was massive: the random static turned into crystal-clear digits extremely fast."

SLIDE 6
Title: Model 3: Conditional DCGAN (cDCGAN)
Bullets:
- The Problem: Standard GANs generate random, uncontrollable images.
- The Solution: Injecting 'Class Labels' via nn.Embedding.
- Dataset: Fashion-MNIST (Shoes, Shirts, Bags).
- Result: We can command the network to generate a specific clothing item on demand.
Speaker Notes: "A major flaw with normal GANs is you can't control what it draws. We solved this by building a cDCGAN. We embedded class labels directly into the network. This allowed us to successfully map the complex Fashion-MNIST dataset and say 'generate a shoe', and the network would oblige."

SLIDE 7
Title: Training Dynamics and Loss Functions
Bullets:
- Metric: Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) Loss.
- Discriminator Loss: Hovered healthy around 0.3 - 0.7.
- Generator Loss: Sustained around 1.0 - 2.5.
- Mode Collapse: Successfully avoided.
Speaker Notes: "Unlike normal classification where loss drops to zero, GAN losses must oscillate as the networks fight. If Discriminator loss hits exactly zero, the Generator stops learning. Our graphs showed a perfectly healthy equilibrium, meaning we avoided Mode Collapse—the common trap where a GAN just generates the exact same image forever."

SLIDE 8
Title: Latent Space Ablation Study
Bullets:
- Analyzed the input noise vector dimension (z).
- Tested: z = 50, z = 100, z = 200.
- Result: z=50 lacked variation; z=200 wasted compute time.
- Conclusion: z=100 provides optimal model capacity.
Speaker Notes: "To add research depth, we conducted an ablation study on the starting noise vector. We found that 50 random numbers wasn't enough 'genetic variety' to create diverse images, while 200 was overkill. 100 proved to be the mathematical sweet spot."

SLIDE 9
Title: Quantitative Evaluation (FID Scores)
Bullets:
- Metric: Frechet Inception Distance (FID). Lower is better.
- Vanilla GAN (MNIST): 40.58
- DCGAN (MNIST): 17.43
- cDCGAN (Fashion-MNIST): 144.23
Speaker Notes: "We didn't just use human bias to judge the images. We used FID, the industry standard. Dropping from 40.58 to 17.43 mathematically proved DCGAN is superior. The Fashion score is 144 because the Inception model used for FID scoring is built for HD color photos, not 28x28 grayscale clothing."

SLIDE 10
Title: Verification of Control (cDCGAN Grid)
Bullets:
- Class-wise generation grid proves disentanglement.
- Each row represents a specific, isolated clothing class.
- The noise (style) is preserved while the class changes.
Speaker Notes: "Here we can see our final conditional output. Because each row consistently shows a different piece of clothing while retaining unique styles, it mathematically proves our model successfully disentangled the concept of 'class' from the concept of 'random noise'."

SLIDE 11
Title: Conclusion & Key Takeaways
Bullets:
- Convolutions are strictly necessary for high-fidelity image generation.
- Conditional embeddings successfully solve the 'random output' problem.
- FID scoring is effective but suffers from grayscale domain mismatch.
- The pipeline represents a complete, research-grade generative framework.
Speaker Notes: "In conclusion, this project evolved from a basic Vanilla implementation into a robust research pipeline. We proved convolutional superiority, achieved controlled synthetic generation, and backed our visual findings with hard mathematical metrics. Thank you."
